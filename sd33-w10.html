Local Connection
SD-33 sits in the collar-county suburban belt where national headwinds have most directly eroded Republican margins since 2018. The current D+4.4 generic ballot and 43.3% presidential approval (trending down 0.9 points) mirror the environment that produced DeWitte's narrowing 2022 margin. For the Republican primary specifically, these numbers may be less immediately relevant — primary turnout is driven by base enthusiasm — but the general-election winner will face these headwinds directly. The Illinoize's classification of SD-33 as a top Democratic pickup target reflects these structural trends. ANALYSIS: In districts like SD-33 where Pritzker won by competitive margins in 2022, a D+4.4 generic ballot environment makes the open-seat general election genuinely competitive regardless of which Republican emerges from the primary.
📊 District Deep Dive
Mar 2–8 — District Profile
DistrictIllinois Senate District 33 stretches from Crystal Lake in McHenry County south through Algonquin and Lake in the Hills, through St. Charles and Geneva in Kane County, down to Elburn. The district touches parts of DuPage County as well. It spans three county election jurisdictions — McHenry, Kane, and DuPage — creating administrative complexity for campaign operations and voter outreach.
IncumbentSen. Don DeWitte (R-St. Charles), who announced in July 2025 he would not seek reelection. DeWitte was diagnosed with prostate cancer in January 2026 (early detection, favorable prognosis). He was recognized as a leader on transportation, infrastructure, and energy policy, and was known for fiscal restraint.
Race TypeOpen seat — Republican primary (Breugelmans vs. Penman), Democratic primary (Clark, unopposed).
Flip RatingTop 10 most likely to flip (The Illinoize). Democrats are expected to make a 'major run' at this seat.
TrendHistorically safe Republican. Suburban collar counties have trended toward increased competitiveness since 2018. DeWitte won 2022 with a reduced margin consistent with statewide suburban Republican erosion. Open-seat dynamics amplify vulnerability.
RegistrationIllinois does not register voters by party. Primary ballot pull history serves as the partisan proxy. The district has historically leaned Republican but the margin has narrowed as collar-county suburban voters have shifted in recent cycles.
